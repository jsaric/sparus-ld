Our method detects 18 different landmarks of the gilthead seabream (Sparus aurata).

Abstract

The gilthead seabream training dataset consists of 1,084 adult specimens collected from three distinct origins along the eastern Adriatic Sea as part of the AQUAPOP project (https://jadran.izor.hr/~tsegvic/aquapop/): wild (XXX specimens), farm-associated (XXX specimens), and farmed (XXX specimens). Upon completion of the AQUAPOP project, an additional 720 adult specimens were collected from the same geographical area in the period 2017 to 2022. The dataset consists of 206 farm-associated specimens, 324 farmed specimens, 190 wild specimens of gilthead seabream and was designated as the test set. Although the present study focuses on a single species, the dataset presents a challenge for automatic landmark placement due to the subtle but important morphological differences between the three origins (Šegvić-Bubić et al., 2014; Talijančić et al., 2019; 2021). Wild gilthead seabream typically has an elongated, spindle-shaped body form with a larger head profile and a subterminal positioned mouth while farm-associated specimens have a bulkier body, lower positioned nape and a thicker trunk area. Farmed specimens have the most pronounced morphology, characterized by a stockier body with contracted trunks and head regions. These origin-specific morphological differences are influenced by environmental conditions, feeding behaviour and genetic factors arising from different rearing conditions and selective breeding programs (Šegvić-Bubić et al., 2014; Žužul et al., 2019; Talijančić et al., 2019; 2021) and pose a complex problem that challenges both automated and manual digitization methods in terms of appropriate landmark placement. Classification of gilthead seabream based on their origin is further complicated by the species pronounced plasticity which allows for adaptive phenotypic responses to changing environmental conditions. Such trait by itself sets gilthead seabream apart from other aquaculture-relevant fish species just in terms of the sheer magnitude of possible morphological expressions, e.g. head profiles (Brown, 2004). Consequently, increased variability can obscure the identification of homologous landmarks which represent a critical aspect in the design of any geometric morphometrics study (Zelditch et al., 2012). In the context of exploring intraspecific variation in fish species, the dataset used in the present study can serve as a starting point for robust assessment and comparison of measurement errors in landmark placement between automated machine-learning models and human digitization performance.

Dataset

SPARUS-LD dataset contains 2053 high-resolution images of gilthead seabream (Sparus aurata) specimens annotated with 18 landmarks/keypoints. Additionally, each specimen is associated with one of the three classes according to its origin: wild, farmed, or farm-associated. Descriptions of the landmarks are as follows:

  1. anterior tip of snout at the upper jaw
  2. vertical point above the most anterior point in the eye
  3. anterior insertion of the dorsal fin
  4. last spiny ray of the dorsal fin
  5. posterior insertion of the dorsal fin
  6. dorsal point at the least depth of the caudal peduncle
  7. posterior body extremity
  8. ventral point at the least depth of the caudal peduncle
  9. posterior insertion of the anal fin
  10. anterior insertion of the anal fin
  11. insertion of the pelvic fin
  12. ventral tip of the insertion of the operculum on the lateral profile
  13. point of maximum extension of the operculum on the lateral profile
  14. anterior extremity of the lateral line on the head profile
  15. dorsal insertion of the pectoral fin
  16. ventral insertion of the pectoral fin
  17. the most anterior point in the eye
  18. the most posterior point in the eye

We split the dataset into training, validation and test sets. The table below shows the distribution of origin in each set.
